## Abstract

The gut microbiome has emerged as a critical determinant of health and disease across species. While microbiome research has advanced rapidly in human medicine, its translation into veterinary clinical practice remains in its infancy. This review bridges the gap between microbiome science and veterinary practice by providing clinical veterinarians with a comprehensive overview of current knowledge regarding the microbiome in companion animals, livestock, and aquaculture species. We discuss the microbiome as both a diagnostic and therapeutic tool in veterinary medicine, encompassing biomarker discovery, fecal microbiota transplantation (FMT), probiotics, and emerging artificial microbiome therapeutics. Importantly, we highlight the One Health and One Medicine frameworks, which recognize that the microbiomes of humans, animals, and their shared environments are deeply interconnected—with profound implications for antimicrobial resistance, zoonotic disease transmission, and food safety. By synthesizing recent evidence and identifying translational challenges unique to veterinary settings, this review aims to equip clinical veterinarians with actionable knowledge to integrate microbiome science into daily practice and to foster interdisciplinary collaboration across human and veterinary medicine.

**Keywords:** microbiome, veterinary medicine, One Health, One Medicine, fecal microbiota transplantation, antimicrobial resistance, companion animals, livestock, gut microbiota, interdisciplinary

## 1. Introduction: Why the Microbiome Matters for Veterinarians

The microbiome—defined as the collective genomes of all microorganisms residing in and on a host—has become one of the most dynamic frontiers of biomedical research. In human medicine, large-scale initiatives such as the Human Microbiome Project and MetaHIT have mapped the compositional and functional landscape of the human microbiome, linking its disruption (dysbiosis) to a wide range of disorders including inflammatory bowel disease (IBD), colorectal cancer, metabolic syndrome, and neurological conditions[1,2]. These discoveries have catalyzed efforts to exploit the microbiome for diagnosis, prognosis, and therapy, as recently reviewed by Porcari et al.[3].

However, microbiome science is not confined to human health. The gastrointestinal tracts of companion animals, livestock, poultry, horses, and aquaculture species harbor complex microbial communities that are equally essential for digestion, immune development, metabolic homeostasis, and pathogen resistance[4,5]. Over the past decade, advances in high-throughput sequencing technologies—from 16S rRNA gene amplicon sequencing to shotgun metagenomics—have revealed that the principles governing host–microbiome interactions are broadly conserved across mammalian and non-mammalian species, even as species-specific microbial signatures exist[6,7].

Despite this growing body of knowledge, the integration of microbiome science into veterinary clinical practice has been slow. Several barriers account for this gap: the heterogeneity of microbial communities across animal species and breeds; a relative paucity of large-scale, well-controlled veterinary microbiome clinical trials; the lack of standardized protocols for microbiome analysis in veterinary laboratories; and insufficient training of veterinary practitioners in microbiome concepts and their clinical applications[8].

Moreover, veterinary medicine occupies a unique position at the intersection of human, animal, and environmental health. The One Health framework explicitly recognizes this interconnectedness, positing that the health of people, animals, plants, and the broader environment is inextricably linked[9]. The microbiome serves as a powerful embodiment of the One Health concept: microbial strains, antimicrobial resistance (AMR) genes, and metabolites flow continuously between humans, animals, and their shared habitats[10]. Therefore, understanding animal microbiomes is not only critical for improving animal health and welfare but also for safeguarding public health and environmental sustainability.

This review is written for clinical veterinarians and aims to provide a practical, evidence-based overview of current microbiome science as it pertains to veterinary practice. We cover: (1) the microbiome as a diagnostic tool in veterinary medicine; (2) therapeutic microbiome modulation, including fecal microbiota transplantation (FMT), probiotics, and emerging live biotherapeutics; (3) the One Health and One Medicine paradigms as they relate to microbiome research; (4) antimicrobial resistance at the human–animal–environment interface; and (5) actions needed to accelerate the clinical translation of microbiome science in veterinary medicine. Throughout, we emphasize the interdisciplinary nature of this field, highlighting opportunities for collaboration between veterinary and human medical professionals.

## 2. The Microbiome in Veterinary Practice: Current Landscape and Challenges

### 2.1 The Gut Microbiome Across Veterinary Species

The composition and function of the gut microbiome vary substantially across veterinary species, reflecting differences in anatomy, diet, physiology, and evolutionary history.

**Companion animals (dogs and cats):** The canine and feline gut microbiomes have been extensively studied using both 16S rRNA gene sequencing and shotgun metagenomics. Recent large-scale metagenomic studies have identified hundreds of microbial species-level genome bins unique to companion animals, as well as species shared with humans[11]. The dominant phyla in healthy dogs and cats include Firmicutes, Bacteroidetes, Proteobacteria, Fusobacteria, and Actinobacteria, although their relative proportions differ between species and are influenced by diet, age, breed, and environment[12,13]. Dysbiosis—characterized by reduced microbial diversity and altered community composition—has been associated with chronic enteropathy, IBD, acute diarrhea, obesity, atopic dermatitis, and diabetes in companion animals[14,15].

**Horses:** The equine gastrointestinal tract relies heavily on hindgut fermentation for energy extraction from fibrous diets. The equine microbiome is dominated by Firmicutes and Bacteroidetes, with a rich diversity of cellulolytic and fiber-fermenting taxa. Disruptions to the equine gut microbiome have been linked to colic, colitis, laminitis, and equine metabolic syndrome[16,17]. The equine microbiome is particularly sensitive to dietary changes, stress, and antibiotic administration.

**Ruminants (cattle, sheep, goats):** The rumen microbiome is one of the most complex microbial ecosystems on Earth, harboring bacteria, archaea, protozoa, and fungi that collectively enable the breakdown of plant cell wall polysaccharides. The rumen microbiome directly influences feed efficiency, methane production, and the animal's overall nutritional status[18]. Rumen microbial composition is a target for interventions aimed at improving livestock productivity and reducing greenhouse gas emissions[19].

**Poultry:** The gut microbiome of chickens and other poultry plays critical roles in nutrient absorption, immune maturation, and resistance to enteric pathogens such as *Salmonella* and *Campylobacter*. The ban on antibiotic growth promoters in many countries has intensified research into microbiome-based alternatives to maintain poultry health and performance[20,21].

**Aquaculture species:** Fish and shrimp gut microbiomes influence growth, feed conversion, disease resistance, and environmental adaptation. The aquatic environment creates unique challenges for microbiome management, including exposure to waterborne pathogens and antimicrobials[22,23].

### 2.2 Lost in Translation: Why Microbiome Science Has Not Yet Reached Veterinary Clinics

Despite the accumulating evidence, several challenges hinder the clinical translation of microbiome science in veterinary medicine:

1. **Biological complexity and species diversity:** Unlike human medicine, veterinary practice encompasses dozens of species, each with distinct microbiome compositions and host–microbiome interactions. Findings from one species cannot be readily extrapolated to another.

2. **Methodological heterogeneity:** Studies in veterinary microbiome research employ diverse sequencing platforms, bioinformatic pipelines, and study designs, limiting cross-study comparisons and meta-analyses[24].

3. **Limited clinical trial evidence:** Most veterinary microbiome studies are observational or involve small sample sizes. Randomized controlled trials (RCTs) with clinical primary endpoints remain scarce.

4. **Absence of standardized protocols:** There are no universally adopted guidelines for sample collection, storage, DNA extraction, sequencing, and data analysis in veterinary microbiome research, unlike the emerging standards in human microbiome studies (e.g., STORMS checklist)[25].

5. **Knowledge gap among practitioners:** Many veterinarians lack formal training in microbiome science, making it difficult to evaluate and apply research findings in clinical settings[8].

6. **Regulatory ambiguity:** The regulatory status of microbiome-based therapeutics (e.g., FMT, live biotherapeutics) varies widely across jurisdictions and is often unclear in the veterinary context.

## 3. The Microbiome as a Diagnostic Tool in Veterinary Medicine

### 3.1 Microbiome-Based Biomarkers for Disease Diagnosis and Prognosis

The identification of microbial signatures associated with specific diseases holds considerable promise for veterinary diagnostics. In companion animals, several lines of evidence support the diagnostic potential of gut microbiome profiling:

- **Canine chronic enteropathy:** Studies have identified distinct microbial signatures—including decreased *Faecalibacterium*, *Blautia*, and *Fusobacterium*, and increased *Escherichia-Shigella*—that differentiate dogs with chronic enteropathy from healthy controls[14,26]. The Dysbiosis Index (DI), a quantitative PCR-based panel measuring the abundance of seven key bacterial groups, has been validated as a clinically useful tool for assessing gut dysbiosis in dogs and is commercially available[14].

- **Feline chronic enteropathy and alimentary lymphoma:** Microbiome profiling has shown potential for distinguishing between inflammatory bowel disease and intestinal lymphoma in cats, conditions that are notoriously difficult to differentiate clinically[28].

- **Equine colic and gastrointestinal disease:** Microbial community changes preceding or accompanying colic episodes in horses have been documented, suggesting potential for early warning biomarkers[16].

- **Livestock disease surveillance:** In poultry, shifts in gut microbiome composition have been proposed as indicators of subclinical necrotic enteritis and *Salmonella* colonization, potentially enabling proactive flock management[20].

### 3.2 Microbiome Diagnostics for Predicting Response to Therapy

Analogous to emerging human cancer microbiome diagnostics[3], the gut microbiome may predict therapeutic outcomes in veterinary patients. For example:

- Baseline microbiome composition has been shown to predict clinical response to dietary intervention and immunosuppressive therapy in dogs with chronic enteropathy[29].
- In livestock, pre-treatment gut microbiome profiles have been investigated as predictors of antibiotic efficacy and post-treatment recovery, with implications for antimicrobial stewardship[30].

### 3.3 Direct-to-Consumer and Veterinary Microbiome Testing

The commercial availability of direct-to-consumer (DTC) microbiome testing for pets has expanded rapidly. Companies now offer stool microbiome sequencing for dogs and cats, providing reports on microbial diversity, dysbiosis markers, and dietary recommendations. However, significant concerns parallel those in human DTC testing:

- Methodological variability across providers
- Limited validation against clinical outcomes
- Risk of over-interpretation and unnecessary interventions
- Absence of regulatory oversight

Clinicians should critically evaluate the clinical utility of these tests and counsel pet owners accordingly[31,32]. The development of veterinary-specific standards for microbiome testing, analogous to the recently published international consensus statement on microbiome testing in human clinical practice[31], is urgently needed.

### 3.4 Emerging Technologies

Multi-omics approaches—integrating metagenomics with metatranscriptomics, metabolomics, and metaproteomics—are beginning to provide deeper functional insights into the animal microbiome beyond compositional profiling. Metaproteomics, for example, can identify metabolically active microbial pathways and diet–host–microbiome interactions at a functional level[33]. Machine learning algorithms applied to multi-omics datasets are facilitating the discovery of composite biomarkers with improved diagnostic accuracy[34].

Long-read sequencing technologies (e.g., Oxford Nanopore, PacBio) are increasingly being applied to veterinary microbiome research, enabling strain-level resolution and the detection of mobile genetic elements carrying antimicrobial resistance genes[35].

## 4. The Microbiome as a Therapeutic Target and Tool in Veterinary Medicine

### 4.1 Fecal Microbiota Transplantation (FMT) in Companion Animals

Fecal microbiota transplantation—the transfer of processed fecal material from a healthy donor to a recipient—has become an established therapy for recurrent *Clostridioides difficile* infection in humans[36]. In veterinary medicine, FMT is gaining traction, particularly for companion animals with chronic gastrointestinal disorders.

**Clinical evidence in dogs:** Multiple prospective studies (2023–2025) have investigated FMT for canine chronic enteropathy refractory to standard therapies:

- A large prospective study involving 39 dogs with refractory chronic enteropathy found that 72% responded to repeated rectal FMT, with 13 dogs able to taper corticosteroid therapy. Dogs with less baseline dysbiosis responded better and for longer[37].
- A longitudinal study tracked 20 dogs with chronic enteropathy before and up to one year after FMT. Most showed clinical improvement as measured by the Canine IBD Activity Index (CIBDAI), dysbiosis index, and bile acid profiles. 17 of 20 improved at 3 months, and 10 of 20 maintained improvement at one year[38].
- A 2025 study in the Journal of Veterinary Internal Medicine assessed single FMTs in dogs with chronic enteropathy, demonstrating significant short-term clinical improvement but inconsistent microbiome restoration, highlighting the need for optimized protocols[39].

**Clinical guidelines:** In 2024, the Companion Animal FMT Consortium published the first evidence-based clinical guidelines for FMT in companion animals, addressing donor selection, screening protocols, preparation methods, delivery routes, dosing, and monitoring[40]. FMT is deemed safe, minimally invasive, and well tolerated for chronic gastrointestinal conditions, including IBD, chronic colitis, and antibiotic-associated dysbiosis. Emerging indications include parvoviral enteritis and certain dermatologic conditions.

**FMT in horses:** Evidence for FMT in horses remains limited and largely anecdotal or case-based. FMT has been used for *Clostridioides difficile*-associated colitis and antibiotic-induced dysbiosis in horses, but large-scale prospective trials are lacking[41].

### 4.2 Probiotics, Prebiotics, and Synbiotics

Probiotics—live microorganisms that confer a health benefit when administered in adequate amounts—represent the most widely used microbiome modulation strategy in veterinary practice. However, several caveats apply:

- **Strain specificity:** Not all probiotic strains are equally effective, and efficacy is highly host- and indication-specific. The extrapolation of human probiotic data to animals is not always valid[42].
- **Regulatory heterogeneity:** The regulatory classification of veterinary probiotics varies across jurisdictions, and many commercial products lack rigorous clinical validation.
- **Evidence base:** Probiotics have shown benefit in certain contexts, including prevention of acute diarrhea, management of antibiotic-associated dysbiosis, and immune modulation in companion animals[43]. In livestock, probiotics are being investigated as alternatives to antibiotic growth promoters, with evidence supporting improved feed efficiency, reduced pathogen load, and enhanced immune responses[44].

Prebiotics (non-digestible substrates that selectively stimulate beneficial microbes) and synbiotics (combinations of probiotics and prebiotics) are also being explored, with growing evidence supporting their use in both companion animals and livestock[45].

### 4.3 Emerging Microbiome Therapeutics

**Next-generation probiotics:** Single-strain live biotherapeutics derived from host-associated commensals—such as *Faecalibacterium prausnitzii* and *Akkermansia muciniphila*—represent a new class of microbiome therapeutics that differ from traditional probiotics in their autochthonous nature and mechanistic basis[46]. While most evidence comes from human studies, their veterinary application is being explored.

**Defined bacterial consortia:** In human medicine, donor-derived and defined bacterial consortia (e.g., SER-109, VE303) have been approved or investigated for recurrent *C. difficile* infection and other indications[47,48]. The development of veterinary-specific consortia is at an early stage but represents a promising direction.

**Bacteriophage therapy:** Bacteriophages—viruses that specifically infect and lyse bacteria—offer targeted approaches to eliminate pathogenic bacteria while sparing beneficial commensals. Phage therapy is being investigated in veterinary medicine for multidrug-resistant infections and enteric pathogens, particularly in livestock and aquaculture[49].

**Engineered probiotics:** Genetically modified microorganisms designed to produce therapeutic compounds, deliver vaccines, or perform specific functions within the gut represent an emerging frontier, though their veterinary application remains largely preclinical[50].

### 4.4 Dietary Modulation of the Microbiome

Diet is the single most influential modifiable factor affecting gut microbiome composition. In veterinary practice, dietary interventions targeting the microbiome include:

- **Fiber supplementation:** Dietary fiber modulates short-chain fatty acid (SCFA) production and microbiome diversity. Recent research has highlighted the value of specific fiber types in supporting microbiome health in both companion animals and livestock[51].
- **Novel protein and hydrolyzed diets:** Used in the management of food-responsive enteropathies, these diets also alter microbiome composition[52].
- **Functional foods:** Ingredients such as omega-3 fatty acids, polyphenols, and fermented products are being investigated for their prebiotic and anti-inflammatory effects on the gut microbiome[53].

## 5. The One Health Paradigm: Connecting Human, Animal, and Environmental Microbiomes

### 5.1 One Health, One Microbiome

The One Health concept, endorsed by the World Health Organization (WHO), the World Organisation for Animal Health (WOAH), the Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO), and the United Nations Environment Programme (UNEP), provides a framework for understanding how the health of humans, animals, and ecosystems is interconnected[9]. The microbiome is a powerful lens through which to view this interconnectedness.

A landmark review published in *Microbiome* (2025) introduced the concept of "One Health, One Microbiome," demonstrating that microbial strains are extensively shared across human, animal, and environmental reservoirs[10]. Key findings include:

- **Cross-species microbial transmission:** Pet owners share a significant proportion of their gut and skin microbiome with their companion animals, with cohabitation duration and contact intensity influencing the degree of sharing[54]. Livestock workers similarly share microbial communities with their animals[55].
- **Environmental reservoirs:** Soil, water, and wildlife microbiomes serve as reservoirs for both beneficial commensals and pathogenic microorganisms, connecting agricultural, wildlife, and human health[56].
- **Antimicrobial resistance gene flow:** AMR genes circulate among human, animal, and environmental microbiomes, highlighting the need for integrated surveillance and stewardship[57].

### 5.2 One Medicine: Bridging Human and Veterinary Microbiome Research

The One Medicine concept extends One Health by emphasizing that clinical approaches, therapeutic strategies, and scientific knowledge should be shared and translated across human and veterinary medicine. In the context of the microbiome, this means:

- **Translational research:** Animal models have been foundational for understanding host–microbiome interactions. Germ-free mice colonized with defined microbial communities have elucidated causal mechanisms linking the microbiome to obesity, inflammation, and cancer[58]. Conversely, insights from veterinary clinical studies—such as FMT outcomes in dogs—can inform human clinical trial design.

- **Comparative microbiomics:** Systematic comparison of microbiomes across species is revealing conserved and species-specific microbial–host interaction pathways, supporting the identification of universal therapeutic targets[7].

- **Shared diagnostic tools:** Diagnostic frameworks developed for human microbiome testing can be adapted for veterinary use, and vice versa. For example, the Dysbiosis Index validated in dogs[14] has parallels with human gut microbiome diagnostic panels.

- **Cross-disciplinary clinical trials:** Collaborative clinical trials involving both human and veterinary patients—studying, for example, the impact of household-level antimicrobial use on shared microbiomes—represent a frontier for One Medicine research.

In August 2025, the American Animal Hospital Association (AAHA) released its first One Health Guidelines, providing standardized terminology and communication protocols between medical and veterinary teams, along with strategies for addressing zoonoses, AMR stewardship, and shared public health threats[59]. These guidelines represent a milestone in operationalizing the One Health/One Medicine approach.

### 5.3 The Shared Virome: An Emerging Frontier

Beyond bacteria, the virome—the collection of viruses in a microbial community—is an emerging area of One Health microbiome research. Recent studies have highlighted the exchange of viral communities at the human–animal interface, with implications for zoonotic and reverse zoonotic viral transmission[60]. The COVID-19 pandemic underscored the importance of understanding viral dynamics across species, and the fecal virome is increasingly recognized as a valuable surveillance tool.

## 6. Antimicrobial Resistance: A One Health Microbiome Challenge

### 6.1 The Role of the Animal Microbiome in AMR

Antimicrobial resistance is one of the greatest threats to global health, and the animal microbiome plays a central role in the emergence, selection, and dissemination of resistance genes. Key considerations for veterinarians include:

- **Antimicrobial use in animals:** Therapeutic and prophylactic use of antimicrobials in veterinary medicine exerts selective pressure on gut microbiomes, enriching for resistant bacteria and resistance genes[57,61]. Antibiotics are often excreted unmetabolized, entering soils, water, and air, further propagating resistance in the environment.

- **The veterinary resistome:** Surveillance studies have documented high prevalence of resistance genes—particularly those encoding resistance to tetracyclines, sulfonamides, and β-lactams—in the gut microbiomes of livestock, companion animals, and aquaculture species[62,63].

- **Cross-compartment transmission:** Multidrug-resistant bacteria and their resistance genes can be transmitted between animals and humans via direct contact, food products, and shared environments. Veterinary settings are increasingly recognized as critical nodes in the AMR transmission network[64].

### 6.2 Antimicrobial Stewardship and Microbiome-Based Alternatives

Veterinarians are essential stakeholders in antimicrobial stewardship. Strategies that leverage microbiome science to reduce antimicrobial use include:

- **Microbiome-guided therapy:** Using microbiome diagnostics to identify patients most likely to benefit from antimicrobial treatment, and those for whom alternative approaches (e.g., FMT, probiotics) may be appropriate[30].
- **Competitive exclusion:** Administering defined microbial consortia to prevent pathogen colonization, reducing the need for prophylactic antibiotics in livestock[65].
- **Phage therapy:** Targeted bacteriophage treatment as an alternative to broad-spectrum antibiotics for resistant infections[49].
- **Vaccination and biosecurity:** Optimizing vaccination programs and biosecurity measures to reduce disease incidence and, consequently, antimicrobial use[66].

### 6.3 Integrated One Health AMR Surveillance

Effective AMR surveillance requires integration of data from human, animal, and environmental sources. Metagenomic approaches are being applied to monitor AMR genes across these compartments, informing evidence-based policy and stewardship interventions. The veterinary profession has a critical role to play in advocating for, and contributing data to, integrated One Health AMR surveillance systems[57,64].

## 7. The Microbiome in Specific Veterinary Clinical Contexts

### 7.1 Gastroenterology

Gastrointestinal disorders are the primary clinical context for microbiome research in veterinary medicine. In companion animals, microbiome profiling and FMT are being integrated into the management of chronic enteropathy, acute diarrhea, and antibiotic-responsive diarrhea[14,37,40]. In horses, management of post-antibiotic dysbiosis and colitis represents a growing indication for microbiome-based intervention[16]. In livestock, microbiome management is central to preventing enteric diseases and optimizing feed efficiency[18,20].

### 7.2 Dermatology

The skin microbiome is increasingly recognized as an important factor in veterinary dermatologic health and disease. Canine atopic dermatitis, for example, is associated with shifts in the skin microbial community, including increased abundance of *Staphylococcus* species[67]. Microbiome-based approaches, including topical probiotics and bacteriophage therapy, are being explored as adjunctive treatments for recurrent skin infections[68].

### 7.3 Oncology

Emerging evidence from human oncology suggests that the gut microbiome modulates responses to cancer immunotherapy[69]. While veterinary oncology microbiome research is in its infancy, preliminary studies in dogs have identified microbial signatures associated with specific cancer types, and there is growing interest in exploring whether microbiome modulation can improve cancer treatment outcomes in veterinary patients[70].

### 7.4 Metabolic and Endocrine Disorders

The gut microbiome is closely linked to metabolic health across species. In companion animals, associations between gut dysbiosis and obesity, diabetes mellitus, and hepatic disease have been reported[15,71]. In ruminants, the rumen microbiome is a key determinant of metabolic efficiency and is a target for interventions to improve productivity and reduce metabolic disease[18,19].

### 7.5 Behavioral and Neurological Health

The microbiome–gut–brain axis is a rapidly expanding area of research. In companion animals, preliminary evidence suggests links between gut microbiome composition and behavioral traits, including anxiety and stress-related behaviors[72]. These findings open the possibility of microbiome-based interventions for behavioral conditions, although clinical evidence remains limited.

## 8. Actions Needed to Accelerate the Integration of Microbiome Science into Veterinary Practice

### 8.1 Standardization of Veterinary Microbiome Research

The development and adoption of standardized protocols for sample collection, storage, DNA extraction, sequencing, bioinformatic analysis, and reporting are essential to improve the reproducibility and comparability of veterinary microbiome studies. Existing frameworks from human microbiome research—such as the STORMS checklist[25] and the International Human Microbiome Standards (IHMS) project—can serve as templates for veterinary-specific standards.

### 8.2 Improving the Design of Veterinary Microbiome Clinical Trials

Key improvements include:

- **Larger, multi-center trials:** Collaborations between academic and non-academic veterinary institutions are needed to generate adequately powered studies with generalizable results.
- **Clinical primary endpoints:** Trials should prioritize clinically meaningful outcomes rather than purely microbiological endpoints.
- **Proper statistical methods:** Sample size estimation, correction for multiple comparisons, and rigorous statistical frameworks should be applied to veterinary microbiome studies[73].
- **Integration of multi-omics:** Combining metagenomics with metabolomics, proteomics, and transcriptomics will provide deeper mechanistic insights.

### 8.3 Regulatory Frameworks for Veterinary Microbiome Therapeutics

Clarity is needed regarding the regulatory classification and approval pathways for veterinary microbiome therapeutics, including FMT, live biotherapeutics, and bacteriophage products. Engagement between regulatory agencies, academia, and industry is essential to establish frameworks that ensure safety and efficacy while facilitating innovation.

### 8.4 Education and Training

Integrating microbiome science into veterinary curricula—from undergraduate education to continuing professional development—is critical for building the next generation of microbiome-literate veterinarians. Educational initiatives should:

- Cover the basics of microbiome science, including ecology, sequencing technologies, and bioinformatics
- Provide practical frameworks for interpreting microbiome data in clinical contexts
- Emphasize the One Health dimension of microbiome research
- Include interdisciplinary perspectives from human medicine, environmental science, and public health

Scientific societies such as the World Small Animal Veterinary Association (WSAVA), the American Veterinary Medical Association (AVMA), and the European College of Veterinary Internal Medicine (ECVIM) should invest in microbiome-focused educational programs and interdisciplinary workshops.

### 8.5 Fostering Interdisciplinary Collaboration

The inherently interdisciplinary nature of microbiome science demands collaboration across veterinary medicine, human medicine, microbiology, bioinformatics, ecology, public health, and environmental science. Specific actions include:

- Establishing joint research programs and funding calls spanning human and veterinary microbiome research
- Creating shared databases and bioinformatic resources (e.g., the One Health Microbiome Resource[74])
- Promoting translational research outputs through cross-disciplinary journals and conferences
- Engaging industry partners for the development and investigation of veterinary microbiome therapeutics

---

## 9. Conclusions

The microbiome represents a transformative frontier for veterinary medicine, with profound implications for diagnostics, therapeutics, and public health. Microbiome-based diagnostic tools—from the Dysbiosis Index in dogs to metagenomic biomarker panels—are providing clinicians with new ways to assess gut health and predict treatment responses. Therapeutic microbiome modulation, including FMT, probiotics, and emerging artificial microbiome therapeutics, is offering new options for conditions that are difficult to manage with conventional approaches.

The One Health and One Medicine frameworks underscore that veterinary microbiome research is inseparable from human and environmental health. Antimicrobial resistance, zoonotic disease transmission, and food safety are all domains where the animal microbiome plays a critical role, and where veterinarians are indispensable stakeholders.

However, realizing the full potential of microbiome science in veterinary practice requires concerted action: standardizing research methodologies, strengthening clinical trial evidence, clarifying regulatory pathways, investing in education and training, and fostering interdisciplinary collaboration. By embracing the microbiome as both a scientific concept and a clinical tool, veterinarians can contribute to a healthier future for animals, humans, and the environments they share.
