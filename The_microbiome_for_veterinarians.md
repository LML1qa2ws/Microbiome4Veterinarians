## Abstract

Veterinary microbiome research has reached a useful but uneven stage of translation. The field no longer needs broad claims that “the microbiome matters”; it needs evidence that specifies when microbial measurements alter diagnosis, prognosis, treatment choice, biosecurity, or antimicrobial stewardship. This review therefore focuses on actionable microbiome ecology across companion animals, horses, ruminants, poultry, and aquaculture species. We argue that the strongest veterinary applications are not universal “microbiome optimization” but context-specific interpretation of perturbation, recovery, metabolic function, and microbial traffic between animals, humans, and environments. In dogs, the best-developed evidence concerns chronic enteropathy, acute diarrhea, diet responsiveness, dysbiosis indices, bile acid metabolism, and fecal microbiota transplantation (FMT), although even recent FMT studies show that clinical improvement may occur without consistent community-level engraftment. In cats, the key problem is not the absence of microbial signal but the difficulty of separating inflammatory bowel disease, alimentary lymphoma, diet effects, and age-related comorbidity. In horses, management and antimicrobial exposure dominate interpretation; longitudinal studies show marked drug-specific fecal microbiome disruption, and case-control data link antimicrobial-associated diarrhea with stronger microbial and metabolomic disturbance. In ruminants and poultry, microbiome work is most mature when embedded in production endpoints such as feed efficiency, methane yield, pathogen exclusion, and antimicrobial resistance. In aquaculture, host microbiomes must be interpreted together with water and feed microbiomes. Across species, taxonomic profiles alone are rarely sufficient; metabolomics, absolute quantification, strain tracking, resistome analysis, and well-curated metadata are increasingly necessary. We conclude with a focused framework for veterinary microbiome studies and clinical use: define the decision, define the perturbation, measure function where possible, avoid overinterpreting fecal taxonomy, and prioritize controlled intervention studies with clinically meaningful endpoints.

**Keywords:** veterinary microbiome; dysbiosis; chronic enteropathy; fecal microbiota transplantation; antimicrobial resistance; One Health; metabolomics; companion animals; livestock; aquaculture

## 1. Introduction: Why Veterinary Microbiome Science Needs a Narrower Claim

Microbiome science has become a familiar language in veterinary medicine, but its clinical meaning remains inconsistent. The problem is not that microbial communities are unimportant. The problem is that many veterinary claims still move too quickly from association to action. A fecal microbial profile that differs between diseased and healthy animals does not automatically diagnose disease, identify causality, justify a supplement, or predict response to treatment. This distinction is central for veterinarians because animal patients differ not only by species but also by digestive anatomy, husbandry, diet, antimicrobial exposure, housing density, environment, and the clinical purpose of testing.

The most useful veterinary microbiome question is therefore not, “What is the normal microbiome?” A more clinically useful question is, “Which microbial function or ecological state changes a decision in this animal, herd, flock, or aquatic system?” This review adopts that narrower question. We use *dysbiosis* only as a descriptive term for a disturbed microbial ecosystem, not as a diagnosis. We also distinguish microbial composition from microbial function. Relative abundance plots may describe a community, but they do not directly measure microbial load, metabolite output, pathogen exclusion, antimicrobial resistance (AMR) mobility, or durability after intervention.

This review is structured in six sections, as appropriate for a focused *Animals* review: (1) the translational standard required for veterinary microbiome evidence; (2) species-specific microbiome landscapes and clinical implications; (3) diagnostics and intervention strategies; (4) One Health and the animal resistome; (5) study-design priorities; and (6) conclusions. The emphasis is on primary research where available, with review and consensus papers used only when they clarify standards or regulatory context.

## 2. What Counts as Actionable Veterinary Microbiome Evidence?

### 2.1. From taxonomic difference to clinical utility

A microbiome result becomes clinically useful only when it changes interpretation or management beyond what the veterinarian already knows from history, examination, routine clinicopathology, imaging, pathology, or production records. In companion animals, this may mean tracking chronic enteropathy activity, identifying post-antibiotic perturbation, or supporting selection of diet or FMT in carefully defined cases. In livestock and poultry, it may mean identifying microbial functions associated with feed conversion, methane yield, pathogen colonization, necrotic enteritis risk, or AMR burden. In aquaculture, microbiome data are useful only when interpreted as part of a water-feed-host system rather than as an isolated intestinal profile.

The evidence hierarchy should therefore be stricter than simple case-control association. Cross-sectional 16S rRNA sequencing can identify candidate patterns, but it is weak evidence for intervention. Stronger evidence comes from longitudinal sampling, experimentally controlled diet or antimicrobial exposure, metagenomics or metabolomics linked to host outcomes, and intervention studies with prespecified clinical or production endpoints. The canine literature illustrates this well. A dysbiosis index based on qPCR improves standardization compared with open-ended taxonomic description, but its correct role is adjunctive: it identifies a disturbed fecal bacterial pattern and can be trended, but it does not explain why the disturbance occurred [1]. Metabolomic studies in dogs with chronic or acute gastrointestinal disease add more direct biological meaning because bile acids, short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs), and other host-microbial metabolites are closer to epithelial signaling, motility, and inflammatory tone than a genus-level abundance plot [2,3].

### 2.2. Function is often more informative than membership

Microbiome studies often report alpha diversity, beta diversity, and relative abundance. These metrics are useful but insufficient. Increased diversity is not always beneficial, and reduced diversity is not always pathological. A high-functioning rumen, cecum, or poultry cecal ecosystem may be specialized rather than maximally diverse. Conversely, a community can appear taxonomically stable while its metabolite output, AMR gene carriage, or pathogen-resistance capacity changes.

Functional readouts are particularly important in four veterinary contexts. First, bile acid transformation is relevant to canine and feline enteropathy because microbial deconjugation and conversion of primary to secondary bile acids can affect epithelial signaling, motility, and pathogen resistance [2,4]. Second, SCFA production is central in horses, ruminants, poultry, and fish because it links substrate flow to host energy and mucosal function. Third, resistome analysis is essential when antimicrobial exposure, herd-level stewardship, hospital environments, or food-chain interfaces are under consideration [5–7]. Fourth, strain tracking matters for FMT, probiotics, phages, and transmission studies because genus-level similarity does not prove engraftment, persistence, or mobility.

### 2.3. Sampling and metadata decide interpretability

Veterinary samples are not interchangeable. Feces, rectal swabs, mucosal biopsies, rumen fluid, cecal content, skin swabs, milk, litter, tank water, and feed each answer different questions. Feces are convenient but may poorly represent mucosa or proximal gut compartments. In livestock and poultry, pen, flock, barn, diet batch, litter, and water-line effects can exceed individual-animal effects. In horses, management and diet history can dominate fecal microbial variation. In aquaculture, host-associated microbial data are difficult to interpret unless water quality, system design, feed, stocking density, and pathogen pressure are recorded.

At minimum, veterinary microbiome studies should report species, breed or line, age, sex/neuter status where relevant, diet composition and recent changes, antimicrobial/probiotic/prebiotic exposure, housing, geography, clinical phenotype, sampling compartment, storage conditions, DNA/RNA extraction method, sequencing region or platform, bioinformatic workflow, contamination control, and statistical handling of compositionality. The STORMS checklist was developed for human microbiome studies but its logic is directly relevant to veterinary research [8].

**Table 1. Evidence-focused interpretation of common veterinary microbiome approaches.**

| Approach | Best question answered | Strength | Weakness that commonly causes overclaiming | Veterinary use most likely to be defensible |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 16S rRNA amplicon sequencing | Which broad bacterial groups differ? | Scalable and inexpensive | Limited species/strain resolution; weak functional inference; compositional bias | Discovery cohorts, surveillance, longitudinal disturbance tracking |
| qPCR panels / dysbiosis indices | Are predefined bacterial targets shifted? | Standardized, quantitative, trendable | Narrow target set; cannot diagnose cause | Canine chronic enteropathy monitoring, post-antibiotic recovery tracking |
| Shotgun metagenomics | Which taxa, genes, pathways, strains, and AMR genes are present? | Higher resolution; supports resistome and strain analysis | Cost, host DNA, bioinformatics burden | AMR surveillance, FMT engraftment, pathogen exclusion, production-system ecology |
| Metabolomics | Which microbial or host-microbial metabolites changed? | Closer to phenotype and mechanism | Sensitive to storage, diet, and host metabolism | Bile acid and SCFA studies, enteropathy phenotyping, feed-efficiency research |
| Culture plus WGS | Which viable isolate or strain is present? | Essential for strain identity, safety, and AMR linkage | Misses uncultured organisms | Probiotic validation, pathogen surveillance, phage targeting, hospital outbreaks |
| Long-read sequencing | How are AMR genes, plasmids, and genomes linked? | Better assembly and mobile-element resolution | Higher cost and analysis complexity | Plasmid-resolved resistome work, One Health transmission studies |

## 3. Species-Specific Landscapes and Clinical Implications

### 3.1. Dogs: the clearest clinical signal, but still not a stand-alone diagnostic

Dogs are the best-developed veterinary model for companion-animal gut microbiome translation. Three features make canine work unusually informative. First, dogs develop naturally occurring chronic enteropathies with clinical heterogeneity similar to human inflammatory bowel disease but embedded in real-world diet, breed, and owner environments. Second, canine fecal microbiome studies have repeatedly reported decreased obligate anaerobes and increased facultative anaerobes in acute diarrhea and chronic enteropathy [2,3,9]. Third, dogs now have standardized tools such as the qPCR-based dysbiosis index [1].

The strongest canine message is that chronic enteropathy should be interpreted as an ecosystem-function problem, not simply as “low diversity.” Dogs with idiopathic inflammatory bowel disease showed altered fecal microbiota and serum metabolites, including changes consistent with disturbed microbial metabolism [2]. Dogs with acute diarrhea also showed microbial dysbiosis and metabolomic changes [3]. These studies support the view that the clinically relevant signal is not a single missing taxon but a disturbance involving anaerobic fermentation, bile acid conversion, and inflammatory context.

Diet is a major, reproducible driver. Shotgun metagenomics in 129 dog fecal metagenomes produced a dog gut gene catalog and showed that diet had a large and reproducible effect on the dog microbiome independent of breed or sex [10]. This matters clinically because diet trials remain first-line management for many dogs with chronic enteropathy. It also means that microbiome tests interpreted without recent diet history are weak.

FMT is the most visible canine microbiome therapy, but recent studies argue for caution rather than enthusiasm. In seven dogs with chronic inflammatory enteropathy, a single rectal FMT was associated with short-term clinical improvement, yet consistent changes in recipient microbiota composition or diversity were not observed [11]. In 20 dogs with chronic enteropathy refractory to diet, FMT improved clinical index, dysbiosis index, fecal bile acids, and propionate at one month; however, alpha- and beta-diversity did not clearly separate diseased dogs from healthy controls either before or after FMT [12]. A 2025 study reported that repeated FMT in dogs with chronic enteropathy can decrease disease activity and corticosteroid use [13]. Together, these studies suggest that FMT may help selected dogs, but the mechanism may involve functional shifts, metabolites, donor effects, or host response rather than simple taxonomic normalization.

**Clinical implication:** for dogs, microbiome testing is most defensible as an adjunct in chronic or recurrent gastrointestinal disease, especially when combined with diet history, clinical activity scores, fecal markers, cobalamin/folate status where relevant, and bile acid or metabolomic data. It should not be marketed as a stand-alone diagnostic for “gut health.”

### 3.2. Cats: signal exists, but phenotype separation is the bottleneck

Feline microbiome research is less mature than canine research, partly because cats present a harder phenotyping problem. Feline chronic enteropathy commonly includes inflammatory bowel disease and alimentary small cell lymphoma, two conditions that may overlap clinically and histologically. A Scientific Reports study of 38 healthy cats and 27 cats with chronic enteropathy characterized fecal microbiome differences in inflammatory bowel disease and small cell lymphoma, supporting the presence of microbial signal but not replacing histopathology or clonality assessment [14].

More recent feline work emphasizes function. A BMC Veterinary Research study of 16 cats with chronic enteropathy and 14 controls found lower alpha and beta diversity in diseased cats, higher Proteobacteria, Enterobacterales, Enterobacteriaceae, and *Escherichia-Shigella*, and altered fecal fatty acid profiles [15]. A hydrolyzed-protein diet study used untargeted nuclear magnetic resonance metabolomics to characterize plasma, fecal, and urine metabolic phenotypes in cats with chronic enteropathy [16]. A 2024 serum metabolomics study further showed that naturally occurring feline chronic enteropathies have systemic metabolic signatures before and after medical intervention [17].

FMT and direct-to-consumer microbiome approaches in cats remain exploratory. A 2023 study reported microbiome responses to FMT in cats with chronic digestive issues, but the evidence base remains too small and methodologically heterogeneous for routine recommendations [18].

**Clinical implication:** in cats, microbiome and metabolome data are most useful as research tools for better phenotyping chronic enteropathy and treatment response. They should not be used to distinguish inflammatory bowel disease from small cell lymphoma without conventional diagnostics.

### 3.3. Horses: management and antimicrobial exposure are the clinical frame

The horse is a hindgut fermenter; therefore, microbial ecology is inseparable from forage access, starch load, meal pattern, transport, hospitalization, antimicrobial exposure, and colic/colitis risk. The equine microbiome is clinically important because small management changes can alter fermentation and because antimicrobial-associated diarrhea can be life-threatening.

Two recent equine antimicrobial studies are particularly useful because they move beyond static association. A matched case-control study in *Animals* compared horses with antimicrobial-associated diarrhea, antibiotic-treated controls, and non-treated controls. Antimicrobial exposure reduced richness and evenness, and horses with antimicrobial-associated diarrhea had stronger microbiome and metabolome separation, including altered tryptophan-related metabolites [19]. A prospective longitudinal study of 20 healthy horses showed that oral doxycycline, erythromycin, and metronidazole, but not sulfadiazine/trimethoprim to the same extent, reduced richness and diversity at multiple time points, with increases in *Fusobacterium* and *Escherichia-Shigella* and decreases in fibrolytic and anaerobic taxa [20].

Dietary starch and fiber remain central. Studies of high-fiber versus high-starch feeding and models of oligofructose-induced laminitis show that hindgut microbial shifts should be interpreted as part of substrate overload, acidification, and host inflammatory response rather than as isolated bacterial changes [21,22].

**Clinical implication:** equine microbiome interpretation should begin with the perturbation history: diet transition, forage restriction, transport, hospitalization, and antimicrobial class. The most immediate clinical value is risk awareness and stewardship, not routine microbiome screening.

### 3.4. Ruminants: microbiome medicine is production medicine

In ruminants, the rumen microbiome is not merely associated with digestion; it performs digestion. Consequently, the translational endpoints are different from those in companion animals. Feed efficiency, milk protein yield, nitrogen use, methane emission, weaning transition, neonatal colonization, and AMR burden are often more relevant than individual clinical diagnosis.

Multi-omics studies now link rumen microbial functions to feed efficiency in dairy cattle [23,24]. A 2024 Scientific Reports study integrated host genome and microbiome networks underlying feed efficiency in dairy cows [25]. The methane field is especially active. A 2024 Nature Communications study assembled a metagenomic catalogue of 998 ruminant gut archaeal genomes from 2270 metagenomic samples across ten ruminant species, expanding the genomic basis for understanding methanogenesis [26]. A 2025 Nature Communications study reported that protozoal populations drove system-wide variation in the rumen microbiome in beef cattle, reinforcing that methane and feed-efficiency phenotypes emerge from networks rather than single organisms [27].

**Clinical implication:** for ruminants, the strongest microbiome applications are herd-level and function-led. Microbiome intervention should be evaluated against measurable outputs such as dry matter intake, residual feed intake, milk solids, methane yield, health events, and AMR selection, not only shifts in relative abundance.

### 3.5. Poultry: population-level ecology, pathogen exclusion, and antibiotic-free production

Poultry microbiome work is operationally important because it intersects with feed conversion, necrotic enteritis, litter management, *Salmonella* and *Campylobacter* control, and antibiotic-free production. The flock, house, hatchery, feed, litter, and processing chain are often the relevant units of interpretation.

A 2023 Microbiome study linked cecal SCFAs and host genetics with feed efficiency in broiler chickens [28]. A 2024 Journal of Translational Medicine study tested whether jejunal microbiome composition could help predict clinical and subclinical necrotic enteritis caused by *Clostridium perfringens* [29]. Recent intervention studies also show that alternatives to in-feed antibiotics must be judged by performance and disease endpoints, not only microbial shifts. A 2023 Poultry Science synbiotic study used an experimental necrotic enteritis challenge and measured body weight gain, feed conversion, lesion scores, and related outcomes [30]. A 2026 npj Biofilms and Microbiomes study reported that engineered *Limosilactobacillus reuteri* strains delivering nanobodies against *C. perfringens* toxins improved broiler productivity under subclinical necrotic enteritis and were associated with microbiome and transcriptome changes [31].

**Clinical implication:** in poultry, microbiome work is most valuable when it supports flock-level decisions about competitive exclusion, feed additives, necrotic enteritis control, foodborne pathogen reduction, and AMR surveillance.

### 3.6. Aquaculture: the microbiome includes water and feed

Aquaculture challenges the terrestrial habit of treating the host gut microbiome as a closed compartment. Fish and shellfish continuously interact with water, biofilms, sediment, feed, and tank or pond infrastructure. Thus, “fish microbiome” interpretation without water and system metadata is incomplete.

Recent studies show both opportunity and caution. Inter-species microbial transplantation in fish demonstrated that diet strongly affects gut microbial outcomes after transplantation [32]. Synbiotic supplementation with fructooligosaccharide and *Bacillus subtilis* promoted disease resistance, although not necessarily growth performance, in fish [33]. Aquaculture also has a One Health AMR dimension: a 2024 Microbiome study used metagenomics to examine mobility and co-occurrence of antibiotic resistomes between non-intensive aquaculture environments and humans [34].

**Clinical implication:** aquaculture microbiome interventions should be evaluated as water-feed-host management tools. Probiotic or synbiotic claims require species-specific, pathogen-specific, and system-specific evidence.

| Species/group | Strongest evidence signal | What should be measured with microbiota | Practical interpretation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Dogs | Chronic enteropathy, acute diarrhea, diet response, FMT | Clinical activity score, diet history, dysbiosis index, bile acids, SCFAs, medications | Adjunctive GI phenotyping and monitoring; not a stand-alone diagnosis |
| Cats | Chronic enteropathy and IBD/SCL overlap | Histopathology/clonality when indicated, diet, fecal fatty acids, serum/fecal metabolomics | Research-grade phenotyping; avoid replacing conventional diagnostics |

| Aquaculture | Water-feed-host disease ecology | Gut, water, biofilm, feed, water quality, pathogen pressure | System-level microbiome management |

## 4. Diagnostics and Interventions: What Is Defensible Now?

### 4.1. Diagnostics: use only when a result can change a decision

A veterinary microbiome test should answer a prespecified question. “Is this animal healthy?” is usually too broad. More defensible questions include: Is a dog with chronic enteropathy showing persistent fecal dysbiosis after treatment? Has a horse’s fecal microbiome been strongly disrupted after antimicrobials? Are AMR genes enriched in a farm, flock, or hospital environment? Is a feed additive associated with improved performance and a plausible functional shift?

Commercial microbiome tests often overstate precision. Reports that rank taxa as “good” or “bad” without species-specific validation, diet history, medication history, reference population transparency, and demonstrated outcome improvement are not yet clinical decision tools. International consensus on microbiome testing in clinical practice has similarly emphasized analytical validity, clinical validity, and clinical utility [35]. Veterinary use should follow the same logic.

### 4.2. Diet, prebiotics, probiotics, and synbiotics

Diet is the most practical microbiome intervention because it changes substrate flow. In dogs, metagenomic evidence shows a strong dietary effect [10], and diet trials remain first-line in many chronic enteropathy cases. In cats, hydrolyzed diet studies are beginning to connect diet with metabolomic change [16]. In horses, starch restriction and forage-first feeding are more biologically credible than generic microbial supplements when the problem is hindgut substrate overload. In ruminants and poultry, diet is inseparable from production traits.

Probiotics and synbiotics require strain-, dose-, host-, and indication-specific evidence. A product shown to alter fecal taxa in one species or life stage should not be assumed to improve disease outcomes in another. Poultry and aquaculture studies increasingly evaluate synbiotics against challenge models and performance endpoints [30,33]. That is the correct direction for veterinary microbiome intervention research.

### 4.3. FMT

FMT is attractive because it transfers a community rather than a single organism, but it is not automatically ecological restoration. Donor variability, route, storage, cryoprotectants, recipient niche, diet, antimicrobial exposure, and disease activity all influence outcome. Recent canine studies show clinical promise but also mechanistic uncertainty [11–13]. The evidence supports considering FMT in selected refractory gastrointestinal cases under protocolized conditions, with donor screening and follow-up, rather than routine use for vague “dysbiosis.”

### 4.4. Phages, defined consortia, and engineered live biotherapeutics

Bacteriophages, defined microbial consortia, and engineered live biotherapeutics offer more precision than whole-stool transfer. Human recurrent *Clostridioides difficile* trials show that defined microbiome therapeutics can be clinically effective [36,37], but veterinary translation must consider species, regulation, manufacturing, environmental release, and food-chain safety. Poultry work using engineered *Limosilactobacillus* strains illustrates the future direction: targeted microbial delivery linked to productivity, lesion, microbiome, and host transcriptome endpoints [31].

**Table 3. Intervention claims that are currently defensible versus premature.**

| Intervention | Defensible claim | Premature claim | Minimum evidence needed for stronger use |
|---|---|---|---|
| Diet change | Major modulator of gut microbial function and clinical signs in selected GI diseases | Universal microbiome optimization | Controlled diet trials with metabolite and clinical endpoints |
| Probiotics | May help specific indications when strain, dose, and product quality are known | Any probiotic restores healthy microbiome | Strain-resolved trials with outcome endpoints |
| Prebiotics/synbiotics | Can alter substrate flow and fermentation ecology | Benefits are transferable across species | Species- and diet-specific controlled studies |
| FMT | Promising in selected canine chronic enteropathy cases | General treatment for dysbiosis | Donor screening, controlled trials, engraftment/function tracking |
| Phages | Plausible targeted pathogen control | Broad microbiome-safe antibiotic replacement | Pathogen-specific efficacy, resistance monitoring, regulatory clarity |
| Defined consortia/LBPs | Future precision platform | Routine veterinary therapy now | Manufacturing, safety, colonization, and efficacy data |

## 5. One Health and the Animal Resistome

The animal microbiome is a reservoir and conduit for AMR genes. This is not limited to pathogens. Commensals, opportunists, plasmids, phages, manure, litter, soil, water, feed, hospital surfaces, and wildlife interfaces all contribute to gene flow. Livestock-farm studies have shown environmental remodeling of human gut microbiota and antibiotic resistomes in farm settings [5]. Slaughter-pig and broiler studies demonstrate that fecal resistomes vary across production systems and countries [6]. Aquaculture studies now extend this concern to water-linked environments and human interfaces [34].

Microbiome-informed stewardship expands the clinical frame. Traditional stewardship asks whether the drug, dose, duration, and indication are appropriate. Microbiome-informed stewardship also asks whether the regimen unnecessarily disrupts colonization resistance, enriches AMR genes, affects herd or flock resistome burden, or creates downstream environmental exposure. This perspective is particularly relevant in equine antimicrobial-associated diarrhea, poultry antibiotic-free production, ruminant manure systems, aquaculture, and referral hospitals.

The practical veterinary contribution is not to sequence every patient. It is to identify settings where microbiome or resistome surveillance changes policy: recurrent hospital outbreaks, high-risk antimicrobial use, post-weaning or post-hatch disease programs, manure management, aquaculture water discharge, and integrated One Health surveillance.

## 6. Study-Design Priorities and Conclusions

### 6.1. Priorities for the next generation of veterinary microbiome studies

Veterinary microbiome studies should be redesigned around decisions and mechanisms. We propose six priorities.

1. **Define the clinical or production decision before sampling.** A study of chronic enteropathy, feed conversion, necrotic enteritis, colitis, or AMR surveillance should state what action the microbiome result could change.
2. **Phenotype deeply.** Disease labels such as chronic enteropathy, diarrhea, colitis, or poor performance are too broad without diet, medication, environment, and host data.
3. **Measure function when possible.** Bile acids, SCFAs, metabolomics, metagenomic pathways, resistomes, and strain tracking should be included when they match the question.
4. **Use longitudinal and interventional designs.** Cross-sectional associations should be treated as discovery, not clinical proof.
5. **Report methods transparently.** Sampling, storage, extraction, sequencing, bioinformatics, and contamination control must be detailed enough to reproduce.
6. **Publish negative and mixed results.** FMT studies that improve clinical signs without consistent engraftment are not failures; they refine the mechanism and prevent simplistic claims.

### 6.2. Conclusions

Veterinary microbiome science is most useful when it stops trying to sell a universal healthy microbiome and instead explains perturbation, function, and context. Dogs currently provide the strongest companion-animal evidence, especially in gastrointestinal disease, but even there microbiome data should complement rather than replace clinical reasoning. Cats require better phenotype separation and functional integration. Horses demonstrate why antimicrobial class, diet, and management history are indispensable. Ruminants and poultry show that microbiome research can be powerful when tied to production, methane, pathogen exclusion, and AMR endpoints. Aquaculture reminds the field that host microbiomes are sometimes inseparable from environmental microbiomes.

The next phase should be less descriptive and more accountable: fewer broad claims, more controlled interventions, more functional readouts, more strain and resistome resolution, and clearer links to decisions that improve animal health, production sustainability, and One Health stewardship.
